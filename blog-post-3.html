The Ides of March

Et Tu, Brute? — What Really Happened on the Ides of March

9 min read 15 March 44 BC Political History

On the morning of 15 March 44 BC — the date the Roman calendar called the Ides of March — Julius Caesar walked into the Theatre of Pompey in Rome and never walked out. Within minutes, 23 senators had stabbed him a total of 23 times. The conspirators called themselves the Liberatores. History has never quite agreed on what they liberated, or whether they achieved it.

The assassination is one of the most documented events of the ancient world, yet it remains one of the most misunderstood. Strip away Shakespeare's poetry and Hollywood's dramatisation, and what emerges is something far more complex: a political crisis years in the making, a web of personal betrayals, and a killing that solved nothing while changing everything.

"Et tu, Bruté?" — "And you, Brutus?"

— William Shakespeare, Julius Caesar, Act III (44 BC, attributed)

The Warning Signs

Caesar was not naive. Roman politics in 44 BC was saturated with tension, and he had been warned repeatedly. The soothsayer Spurinna had cautioned him to "beware the Ides of March" — a warning dismissed as superstition. His wife Calpurnia dreamed of his death and begged him to stay home. A message naming the conspirators was pressed into his hand as he entered the theatre, but he tucked it unread beneath his arm.

Whether Caesar was reckless or fatalistic is debated still. Some historians argue he had grown weary — that a man who had crossed the Rubicon, conquered Gaul, and survived the civil war had lost his instinct for self-preservation. Others suggest he was simply, catastrophically confident: the most powerful man in Rome did not expect that power to be snatched away in a marble corridor.

The Men Who Killed Him

Sixty senators were implicated in the conspiracy to varying degrees. The two principal architects were Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus — men Caesar had pardoned after Pharsalus, elevated, and trusted. The irony was not lost on ancient observers.

Marcus Brutus

Caesar's close friend and protégé. Ideologically opposed to monarchy, he joined the conspiracy out of stated republican principle. Ancient sources suggest Caesar may have been his biological father.

Gaius Cassius

The plot's chief organiser. A brilliant general who harboured deep personal resentment — Caesar had promoted Brutus above him, despite Cassius being the superior soldier.

Decimus Brutus

Perhaps the most damaging betrayal. Caesar's close personal friend and a trusted general, it was Decimus who persuaded a hesitant Caesar to attend the Senate that morning despite his wife's fears.

Gaius Trebonius

Tasked with detaining Mark Antony in conversation outside the building, preventing Caesar's most loyal supporter from intervening.

The Theatre of Pompey

The location was deliberately chosen. The Senate was not meeting in the Curia — which was under renovation — but in the Porticus of Pompey, adjacent to the great theatre that bore Caesar's defeated rival's name. There was a certain theatre to the conspirators' selection: Caesar would die beneath a statue of Pompey.

When Caesar arrived, the senators crowded around him under the pretence of presenting a petition — to recall the exiled Publius Tillius Cimber. As Caesar refused and turned away, Cimber grabbed his toga. That was the signal. Publius Servilius Casca struck the first blow to the neck. Caesar seized Casca's arm. Then the rest descended.

The ancient physician Antistius later examined the body and concluded that only one wound, the second stab to the chest, was fatal. Twenty-two of the twenty-three blows were survivable. The conspirators, in their frenzy, had even wounded each other. Caesar fell at the base of Pompey's statue, covering his face and pulling his toga down over his legs so that he would die with dignity.

"He fell at the base of Pompey's statue and lay there, until his friends came and carried him home in a litter, with one arm hanging down."

— Plutarch, Life of Caesar

What the Conspirators Got Wrong

The Liberatores had planned the assassination meticulously. What they had not planned was what came next. They marched to the Capitoline Hill expecting to be celebrated as heroes who had saved the Republic. The people of Rome did not applaud. They hid behind locked doors.

Mark Antony, Caesar's co-consul and closest ally, was alive and free. Within three days he had obtained Caesar's private papers and his will — which left generous bequests to every Roman citizen. At Caesar's funeral, Antony delivered a speech of such devastating rhetorical skill that the crowd seized the conspirators' property for improvised funeral pyres and drove Brutus and Cassius from the city.

The Republic the conspirators had killed Caesar to save lasted exactly two more years. By 42 BC, Brutus and Cassius were dead — defeated at the Battle of Philippi by Antony and Caesar's adopted heir, Octavian. What followed was not the restoration of republican government but the systematic dismantling of it. Octavian became Augustus, the first Roman Emperor. Every structure Caesar's killers had sought to prevent came to pass, accelerated by their own act.

The Legend That Grew from the Blood

Caesar's assassination transformed him. In life he had been divisive: adored by soldiers and the urban poor, feared and resented by the senatorial class. In death he became something else entirely. Within two years the Roman Senate had formally deified him — Divus Julius, the Divine Julius. His adopted son Octavian shrewdly styled himself Divi filius, Son of a God, and never let anyone forget it.

The phrase Et tu, Bruté does not appear in any ancient source. It is Shakespeare's invention, first performed in 1599. Yet it has lodged itself so completely in cultural memory that it is now virtually impossible to separate the historical assassination from its literary echo. Caesar's death belongs as much to literature as to history — a fact that would, perhaps, have amused a man who spent much of his life composing his own narrative.

The Ides of March did not save the Roman Republic. It killed it. The conspirators struck down one man and unleashed forces that swept away the constitution they had sworn to protect. Two millennia later, 15 March remains the most famous date in Roman history — a reminder that political violence rarely achieves what its authors intend, and that the consequences of a single morning can echo for centuries.
